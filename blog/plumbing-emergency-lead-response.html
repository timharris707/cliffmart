Plumbers miss emergency calls while they're on jobs. An AI doesn't.

A pipe bursts at 9am. The homeowner calls three plumbers in a row. The first one to actually respond gets the job. I mapped where those calls go during a typical plumber's day — the answer is mostly voicemail. Here's what an AI-driven response setup actually looks like, and what the missed-lead math says about why it matters.

A pipe bursts at 9am. The homeowner calls three plumbers off Google. The first one to actually respond gets the job — and probably gets called for every plumbing issue in that house for the next ten years.

The second and third get nothing.

I've been mapping where emergency plumbing calls actually land during a typical workday, and the answer is mostly voicemail. Not because plumbers are ignoring their phones — because they're physically under sinks, in crawl spaces, or mid-repair when the call comes in. The phone rings, they can't grab it, the caller moves on.

That's the whole problem. And it's expensive.

The Lead Window Is Shorter Than Most Plumbers Realize

Emergency plumbing isn't like replacing a water heater or scheduling a bathroom remodel. It's panic-driven. A burst pipe, a backed-up sewer, a water heater that dies on a Monday morning — these are situations where the homeowner needs someone right now. They're not going to wait three hours for a callback.

In emergency home services, roughly 50% of callers book within 90 minutes of first contact. If you haven't responded by then, they've moved on. For genuinely urgent calls — active flooding, no heat in January, sewage backing up — that window collapses to under 30 minutes.

A solo plumber or small shop with two or three techs is going to miss calls. That's just physics. The question is what happens to those callers after they hit voicemail.

What "Calling Back Later" Actually Costs

I ran the math for a small plumbing operation — three techs, 12-15 jobs a week, average ticket around $420.

If that shop misses 25% of inbound calls on weekdays — conservative for a crew that's mostly on jobs during business hours — that's 3-4 missed leads per week. At a 40% close rate on calls that are actually answered, that's one to two booked jobs per week walking out the door.

One missed job per week at $420 average is roughly $22,000 in annual revenue. Two jobs per week is over $40,000.

Those aren't new customers who failed to find you. Those are customers who found you, decided to call, and you weren't there. The hardest part of acquisition — the moment they chose to dial — already happened. You just missed the window.

The First Thing I Got Wrong

My first pass at solving this was a simple auto-reply: when a call goes to voicemail, fire an immediate SMS that says "Hey, we missed your call — what's going on?" The customer can reply with the issue, the plumber sees the message when they surface, calls back as soon as possible.

The problem I hadn't thought through: that text goes out to every missed call, including sales calls, robocalls, existing customers with non-urgent questions, and people who dialed the wrong number. The plumber would be triaging a noisy inbox instead of prioritizing actual emergencies.

The fix was adding a triage layer. Instead of a generic open question, the auto-reply asks one specific thing: "Is this an emergency or can we schedule a visit?" Emergency replies trigger an immediate notification to the on-call tech. Non-emergency replies go into a queue for callback during natural breaks.

That single distinction — emergency vs. schedulable — changes everything about how the workflow operates. The plumber isn't checking messages every ten minutes; they only get interrupted when it actually warrants it.

What an OpenClaw Setup Looks Like for a Plumbing Shop

The core workflow has four parts:

Missed call detection: When a call hits voicemail, an SMS fires from the business number within 30 seconds. Short and personal: "Sorry I missed you — we're on a job right now. Is this an emergency or can we get you scheduled?" The tone matters. This should feel like a person who just stepped away, not an autoresponder.

Triage routing: If the customer replies, OpenClaw scans the text for urgency signals — words like "flooding," "burst," "no heat," "backing up" trigger the emergency path. Replies like "need a quote" or "thinking about" route to the scheduled path. Ambiguous replies get flagged for the plumber to review within 15 minutes. The model isn't perfect; the fallback is the human.

Emergency path: An immediate push notification fires to the on-call tech's phone — a plain text summary of what the customer said, their callback number, and a one-tap option to call directly. The goal is getting a human on the line within 10 minutes of the original call. That's still faster than most competitors who actually picked up.

Scheduled path: OpenClaw sends back three available time slots pulled from the job board. Customer picks one, the job drops into the schedule, and a confirmation goes out automatically. Zero phone tag. The plumber finds out when it appears on their dispatch board.

The Second Triage Question That Cuts Wasted Dispatches

One thing the basic version misses: a lot of plumbing calls that sound like emergencies aren't — or at least aren't the kind that require dropping everything and driving over.

"The toilet keeps running" is annoying but not urgent. "Water is coming through the kitchen ceiling" is a different situation entirely. Dispatching a tech on emergency pricing to a non-emergency call wastes time and erodes customer trust when they see the bill.

Adding a second question after the customer flags urgency — "Is water actively running or coming in right now?" — filters out roughly half the "emergency" calls that are actually urgent-but-schedulable. The customer still gets a fast response and a same-day appointment. The tech doesn't abandon a job they're mid-way through.

Two questions. That's the entire intelligence layer. Everything else in the workflow is logistics.

After the Job: The Follow-Up Window Nobody Uses

Emergency calls have unusually high follow-up revenue potential, and almost no one acts on it.

When a pipe bursts or a water heater fails, the homeowner is in a mindset where they want to prevent this from happening again. That's the window to mention annual maintenance plans, pressure checks, aging water heaters — things they'd otherwise ignore until the next crisis.

A 72-hour follow-up text captures that window cleanly. Not a sales pitch — a check-in: "Everything holding up okay after the repair Tuesday? We noticed your water heater is about 12 years old. Average lifespan is 10-15 years — worth knowing. Happy to take a look next time we're in the neighborhood." That message, sent automatically to every completed emergency job, books additional work without a single cold call.

The timing matters: 72 hours is long enough that the crisis has passed, but short enough that the experience is still fresh. Same-day follow-ups feel like a cash grab. Three-week follow-ups feel like you forgot about them until you needed something.

The Setup Reality

For a shop using Jobber, ServiceTitan, or even a shared Google Calendar, getting this workflow running is a weekend project. Twilio SMS for a shop fielding 60-80 inbound contacts a week runs roughly $25-40 a month. The OpenClaw workflow isn't complex — a missed call trigger, conditional triage routing, and a handful of SMS templates.

The harder part, honestly, is convincing the plumber to trust it. The instinct is to call back yourself, even if it means doing it at the end of a 10-hour day when the customer has long since booked someone else. "I like talking to customers myself" is a real objection — and it's not wrong. But it's competing with a reality where the phone rang at 9:30am while both hands were occupied.

The workflow doesn't replace the relationship. It holds the door open long enough for the relationship to happen.

The Takeaway

Plumbers don't lose emergency leads because they're bad at their jobs. They lose them because the call comes in at the exact moment they can't answer it, and the caller is already dialing the next number before the voicemail beep finishes.

That's a response time problem, not a quality problem. And response time problems have response time solutions — a system that engages within 30 seconds, qualifies urgency with two questions, and either escalates immediately or books a slot without requiring anyone to stop what they're doing.

The math on missed leads is uncomfortable once you work it out. Most plumbers underestimate by a factor of three — the jobs they know they lost are the ones who left a voicemail. The ones who moved on without a message are invisible. But they called.
