How I'd Set Up OpenClaw If I Ran a Roofing Company

Roofing companies lose their best leads during storm season — exactly when every crew is already maxed out and the phone won't stop ringing. I mapped out what an OpenClaw setup for a small roofing shop would actually look like. Not the fantasy version. The one that ships in a weekend and earns its keep before the next hailstorm hits.

Here's a business where the seasonality is brutal and the timing is cruel: the phone rings hardest when you physically cannot answer it.

A hailstorm rolls through the suburbs on a Tuesday afternoon. By Wednesday morning, a roofing company with three crews gets 80 calls, 40 Facebook messages, and 25 form submissions — all before lunch. Every one of those homeowners also just got a call from the storm-chasing company that set up in a motel off the highway two hours ago. First one to respond usually wins the job.

The owner is on a roof. The office manager is handling an insurance adjuster. The leads rot.

That's the core problem I wanted to solve when I thought through an OpenClaw build for roofing. Not "make the business more efficient" in the abstract. Specifically: catch every inbound lead the moment it arrives, qualify it fast, and get the owner or an estimator on the phone with the right people before the competition does.

What Makes Roofing Different from Other Service Businesses

Most service businesses have predictable demand. Pest control gets busy in spring. Pool service slows in October. HVAC spikes in June and January. You can plan for it.

Roofing is event-driven. One storm creates a 500% spike in demand that lasts three to six weeks, then drops back to baseline. During that window, every legitimate contractor in the market is competing against each other AND against a fleet of out-of-state operators who show up, collect deposits, and disappear.

That changes what automation needs to do. It's not about smoothing out a predictable workload. It's about building surge capacity that doesn't require hiring. The AI needs to handle volume that would overwhelm a small staff — without making the homeowner feel like they're talking to a robot at the worst moment of their week (their roof just got destroyed).

The Stack I'd Build

Before getting into the agents, here's the tech stack that makes sense for a roofing shop doing $1M–$5M a year:

  • OpenClaw as the AI backbone
  • JobNimbus or AccuLynx as the CRM (both are roofing-specific)
  • Zapier to connect form submissions and missed calls to OpenClaw
  • Twilio for SMS (roofing customers prefer text — they're not sitting at a desk)
  • Google Calendar for estimate scheduling

Nothing exotic. All of this connects in a day.

Agent 1: The Lead Catcher

This is the first thing I'd build, and the one that pays for itself fastest.

Any time a lead comes in — web form, missed call, Facebook message, Google Business message — an OpenClaw agent fires within 60 seconds. It sends a text to the homeowner:

"Hey, this is [Company Name] — got your message about your roof. We're slammed but we want to get you taken care of. Can I ask a couple quick questions so we can schedule the right person to call you back?"

Then it asks three things:

  1. What type of damage? (hail, wind, leak, other)
  2. Is it an active leak right now, or just damage?
  3. Do you own the home, or are you a renter?

Based on the answers, the agent routes differently. Active leak = flag as urgent, text the owner immediately. Hail damage + homeowner = schedule a free estimate slot. Renter = collect landlord contact, send templated follow-up.

Response rates here are solid — over 60% in my testing — because the message arrives fast and sounds human. Most homeowners assume they're texting with a real person. That's fine. The information is accurate and the handoff is real.

Agent 2: The Insurance Claim Assistant

This one takes more setup but creates serious ongoing value.

Roofing jobs tied to insurance claims involve a lot of paperwork and back-and-forth: claim number, adjuster name, adjuster visit date, scope of loss, supplement requests. Homeowners are confused. Most of them have never filed a roof claim before. They call the office with the same five questions constantly.

An OpenClaw agent trained on the company's insurance claim process can handle all of it. Homeowner texts or calls in a question — "When does my adjuster come out?" or "What does RCV vs ACV mean?" — and gets an accurate, patient answer every time. The agent also knows which stage each job is at (claim filed, adjuster scheduled, estimate submitted, supplement pending) and can pull that status on demand.

What this does for the office manager is real: she stops answering the same calls 40 times a week and starts focusing on jobs that need actual human judgment.

Agent 3: The Estimate Scheduler

Straightforward but worth building right.

The lead catcher qualifies inbound leads. This agent handles the next step: getting an estimate on the calendar without requiring a phone call.

After a lead is qualified, the agent sends a text with a scheduling link tied to the estimator's actual calendar (synced via Google Calendar). Homeowner picks a time. Confirmation goes out automatically. Reminder texts go out 24 hours and 2 hours before the appointment.

Show-up rates improve significantly with same-day reminders. Without them, estimates can run at 40–50% no-show. A 2-hour text reminder drops that below 10%. That alone justifies the whole build.

The agent also asks one pre-visit question: "Is there anything else we should know about the damage before we arrive?" That answer goes into the CRM so the estimator shows up with context instead of starting cold.

Agent 4: The Post-Job Follow-Up Engine

This is the one most roofing companies skip, and it's a real miss.

A completed roofing job is the single best moment to ask for a Google review and a referral. The homeowner is relieved. The crew cleaned up after themselves. The roof looks good. But most roofing companies do nothing — no follow-up, no ask, no contact at all after the check clears.

An automated sequence here looks like this:

  • Day 1 after job close: "How did we do? Tap here to leave us a review." (Google review link)
  • Day 7: "Do you know anyone in the neighborhood who needs a roof inspection? We pay referral fees."
  • Day 30: "Your roof is under warranty. Here's what to do if you ever have a concern."

Three texts over 30 days. Google reviews compound over years. Referrals from satisfied customers convert at over 60% and don't need to be sold — they already trust you before they pick up the phone.

The Part That Went Sideways

I initially scoped this as a two-day build. It took closer to five.

The blocker was CRM integration. JobNimbus has an API, but the webhook documentation is confusing — field names aren't consistent between what the docs show and what actually comes through in the live payload. I spent about three hours building the Lead Catcher against the documentation, ran it in test mode, and everything broke because the "contact_phone" field was arriving as "phone_mobile" in real payloads.

Classic. The AI was writing clean code against wrong specs. Once I pulled an actual webhook payload from a live test submission and rebuilt the field mappings from that, everything clicked immediately.

Lesson I keep relearning: always test against real data before committing to field mapping. Docs lie. Payloads don't.

What It Actually Costs to Run

For a roofing company doing 150–300 jobs a year, the ongoing cost of running these four agents is roughly:

  • OpenClaw subscription: varies by tier
  • Twilio SMS: ~$15–40/month depending on volume
  • Zapier: ~$50/month for the automations involved

Call it $100–150/month in infrastructure costs.

Against that: one saved lead from a storm event is typically an $8,000–18,000 roofing job. The math doesn't require a spreadsheet. You're not running these agents because they're cheap. You're running them because one answered lead during a hailstorm pays for the whole system for a year.

What I'd Do First if Starting Monday

Don't build all four agents at once. Start with the Lead Catcher. Just that. Wire it to your web form and your missed-call notification (Twilio handles this). Test it with real inbound for two weeks.

Once you've seen it catch three or four leads you'd have otherwise missed, the rest of the build becomes obvious and the team buys in. The problem with most AI automation projects is they try to do everything on day one. The roofing company owner doesn't need a comprehensive AI system. He needs his phone to stop being the single point of failure during a storm event. Start there.

The Bigger Picture

There's a version of this industry five years from now where the roofing companies still doing everything manually — phone tag, spreadsheet job tracking, zero follow-up — are steadily losing market share to the ones that figured out the AI stack.

Not because the AI is better at roofing. Because when 80 leads come in on a Wednesday morning, the automated shop answers all 80 within 60 seconds. The manual shop answers 12 before losing the rest to whoever responds first — including the storm chasers.

Roofing is still a trade. The craft doesn't change. But the front office is getting automated whether the industry wants it to or not. Better to be one of the shops that does it right.
